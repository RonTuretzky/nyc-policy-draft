Introduction

About Decentral Park NYC

A collective of technologists, researchers, and community organizers united by a single question: does a given technology liberate communities or entrench power over them? We are technology generalists—rooted in crypto, shaped by its hard lessons, but loyal to outcomes, not tools.

Our Lens: Liberatory vs. Extractive Tech

Every technology can concentrate power or distribute it. Crypto is our worked example: we have watched the same primitives drive speculation and extraction—and also permissionless access, censorship resistance, and genuine community ownership. We bring that scar tissue to every tool we evaluate, from payment rails and identity to AI and community networks.

Rooted in Crypto, Not Limited by It

Our members built and studied blockchain systems—but the mandate is broader. What would technology policy look like if it were designed for public benefit instead of industry promotion? We carry crypto's lessons about ownership, transparency, and capture into the full stack of municipal technology.

The Big Idea

Distributed Ledgers, Plainly

Strip away the hype and a blockchain is one thing: a shared ledger no single company owns—a public record many parties can write to and verify, with no gatekeeper in the middle. That's not an investment thesis. It's infrastructure.

The Analogy

Roads, water mains, and the power grid are public infrastructure. Payment rails and civic record-keeping should be too. Today a handful of corporations own the rails our economy runs on and charge a toll on every transaction. A distributed ledger lets a city own its own payment rails instead of renting them from Visa and Mastercard.

"Crypto is infrastructure, not just investment. Like roads or water systems, payment rails and governance tools should be public goods—not controlled by corporations extracting value from every transaction."

Why It Matters for a City

  • Ownership: the city—not Wall Street—controls the rails it depends on
  • No gatekeepers: permissionless access, including for the unbanked
  • Verifiability: public rules and finances anyone can independently audit
  • Resilience: no single point of control or failure

The Throughline

Everything that follows comes back to one move: owning the city's payment rails and records rather than renting them from extractive intermediaries.

The Case, In Numbers

Why It Matters for Democratizing Technology

The case for public rails isn't abstract—it's about who pays the toll and who gets locked out today.

The Problem with Centralized Tech

  • Payment rails: Visa/Mastercard extract 2-3% of all commerce
  • Platform dependency: Banks can freeze accounts, apps can be shut down
  • Surveillance: Every transaction tracked, shared with law enforcement
  • Financial exclusion: 1.4 billion adults globally unbanked; many more underbanked

What Distributed Ledgers Enable

  • Permissionless access: No gatekeepers deciding who can participate
  • Transparent governance: Decisions recorded on a public ledger
  • Privacy options: Transactions without blanket surveillance
  • Censorship resistance: No single point of control or failure

A Trust Layer for Government

Rather than building new centralized platforms, distributed ledgers can embed transparency and accountability directly into how public rules and finances operate—creating "credibility environments" around government claims that citizens can verify independently, without new bureaucratic overhead.

Guiding Framework

Our Core Principles

Four questions decide every recommendation in this document:

👥

Who Benefits?

Residents or industry? Low-income New Yorkers or tech investors? Public money should serve the public—full stop.

🎯

Real Problem, or a Headline?

Does this solve something residents actually feel—or is it a promotional stunt? And would simpler tech do the job? Blockchain is rarely the best tool.

🔒

Privacy by Design?

Who can access the data—and can it be handed to police or ICE? Surveillance must be impossible by design, not just by promise.

💰

What's the Opportunity Cost?

Every dollar spent on industry promotion is a dollar not spent on housing. Who has a seat at the table—labor, tenants, consumers, or only industry?

Proposal 1

Participatory Budgeting

Give New Yorkers real decision-making power over public resources—using AI-augmented deliberation to scale honest conversation among thousands of residents, surface where they actually agree, and turn that consensus into funded priorities.

🧭

Why AI-Augmented Polis, Not Quadratic Voting

Polis lets residents vote agree/disagree on short statements; machine learning groups them into opinion clusters and highlights the statements that win support across groups. New AI tools then summarize millions of inputs in minutes. The result rewards bridge-building over loud minorities—and avoids the usability wall that has kept quadratic voting from real adoption.

  • Scales deliberation: thousands deliberate, not just vote
  • Surfaces consensus: finds what most residents already agree on
  • Human-in-the-loop: AI summarizes; people decide

Proven in NYC: Harlem District 9

In 2023, RadicalxChange and Council Member Kristin Richardson Jordan ran the first US online, verifiable participatory budget open to a whole constituency. 80% satisfaction. Top-funded: Baxter affordable housing ($1M), Marcus Garvey Playground ($275K).

  • vTaiwan: Polis deliberation converged thousands on ride-hailing rules the government then adopted
  • Anthropic Collective Constitutional AI: ~1,000 representative Americans, 1,127 statements, 38,252 votes drafted public AI rules via Polis

Applications for NYC

Community Board budgets, capital-project priorities, pension-investment input ($250B+), and community-grant distribution—starting by scaling the Harlem pilot citywide with open-source tooling.

Proposal 2

City Currency System

A stablecoin-based municipal payment system that captures value currently extracted by Visa, Mastercard, and other payment processors—redirecting all fees directly into city funding.

Value Being Captured Today

  • 2-3% transaction fees on every card payment in NYC
  • Billions annually flowing to Visa, Mastercard, payment processors
  • Merchant fees passed on as higher prices for residents
  • Zero city benefit from payment infrastructure it enables

Municipal Alternatives Exist

  • Seoul T-money: City-owned payment system (34% municipal ownership), used for transit, retail, and services
  • Lugano, Switzerland: Accepts Bitcoin/USDT for all municipal taxes and services via "Plan B"

Stablecoin Municipal Model

  • NYC Stablecoin: 1:1 USD-backed municipal currency
  • Yield-bearing: Treasury reserves generate interest for city budget
  • Replace credit card rails: Merchants save 2-3% on every transaction
  • Capture all fees: Transaction fees fund city services, not Wall Street
  • Instant settlement: No 3-day wait for merchant deposits

Key Insight

The city already enables billions in commerce through its infrastructure. A municipal stablecoin lets NYC capture the value of its own economy—transaction fees become city revenue, treasury reserves earn yield, and residents benefit from lower merchant costs.

Proposal 3

Municipal Mesh WiFi

By opening NYCHA rooftops to existing community networks and applying for federal money, we can build a community-owned mesh network that provides affordable Internet access, breaks ISP monopolies by replacing corporate extraction with community-owned infrastructure, and bridges the digital divide. The City's own Internet Master Plan (2020) already endorsed this vision—but it was never implemented.

The Digital Divide Today

  • 69% of NYC has only 1-2 ISP choices; 36.7% of Bronx households lack broadband entirely
  • 7x income disparity in connectivity, with Bronx paying $79.83/month vs $56 in Manhattan
  • The Internet Master Plan found 29% of households lack broadband and 1.5 million New Yorkers have neither mobile nor home connection

Community Networks Already Work

  • NYC Mesh: 2,000+ nodes, $600k/year budget, ~$300/node/year operating costs. The IMP's pricing table already lists NYC Mesh at $0/month
  • Red Hook WiFi: Kept its neighborhood connected through Hurricane Sandy—mesh topology routes around failures automatically

Key Insight

Chattanooga returned 12x on investment ($2.69B from $220M). Harvard: municipal networks are 30-50% cheaper. The City's own IMP projects universal broadband would add $142B to Gross City Product and create 165,000 jobs.

Day 1 Actions

  • Open NYCHA rooftops: Rooftop-access directive for 2,410 NYCHA buildings at zero cost—unlocking 335 developments in the most disconnected areas. The IMP already inventoried 24,000 city-owned rooftops and poles
  • Expedite streetlight permits: Fast-track category for community broadband on 400k streetlights with standardized power
  • End corporate welfare: Big Apple Connect funneled $90M to Spectrum and Optimum for zero infrastructure; LinkNYC missed 41% of kiosks and owes $73M

Year 1 Priorities

  • Stabilize community networks: $36k/yr for Red Hook backhaul; $2M/yr grants to NYC Mesh
  • Deploy 50 public-building supernodes: Schools, libraries, fire stations as mesh hubs—~$50k total, reaching 25,000+ residents at $2/household
  • Build on the new direction: the administration's May 2026 $2M "Neighborhood Internet" expansion with Rep. Torres is a foothold to scale community-owned broadband
Part II

Financing the Vision — Public Money, Reimagined

The proposals above cost money to build. NYC raises that money in a ~$4 trillion municipal-bond market—and moves ~$140B/year through its books. The honest finding, after 95+ primary sources and 3-vote verification: switching government to crypto rails does not save billions. NYC's big flows are already cheap; the real value of tokenization is programmability, settlement speed, and access—and the bigger prize is owning the financing rail, not renting it.

💸

The big flows are already cheap

~90% of NYC's $140B in disbursements ride ACH/Fedwire at fractions of a cent. The 2% card fee is flat and paid by the resident (~$21M/yr), not the City. And idle cash isn't idle—NYC earns ~$936M/yr; NY State's STIP earns $4.15B/yr at ~5%.

⚡

Where crypto genuinely helps

Not cost—programmability, transparency, and settlement speed. Instant 24/7 settlement, programmable releases and clawbacks, atomic delivery-versus-payment, and fractional retail access. Real benefits, but a different pitch than "save billions."

🏦

The bigger structural prize

The documented savings in public finance come from public ownership of the borrowing vehicle—not blockchain. The endgame combines the two: a tokenized, member-owned public bond bank.

Part II · Recommendation

An Honest NYC Crypto Pilot Portfolio

Ranked by real, capturable impact—not hype. The strongest pilots target where margin actually exists and what crypto uniquely does, and they stay honest about who benefits.

1 · Stablecoin payments for businesses & high-margin collections

The card fee bites hardest on large business payments: a $1M business-tax or permit bill on a card ≈ $20k in interchange. Offer a sub-card-cost instant stablecoin lane at CityPay and business portals—business income/sales tax, commercial property tax, permits, large fines, court settlements. Margin scales with ticket size.

Margin captured: high (on card-paying businesses) · Beneficiary: payer + shared · Feasibility: medium

2 · Stablecoin as a programmable ACH alternative

Prove a rail with instant 24/7 settlement, programmable releases, and no return/reversal window. ACH is already near-free, so the case rests on speed and programmability—measure settlement time, all-in cost, and error rate against ACH on a controlled flow before any rollout claim.

Cost vs ACH: ~parity · Speed: instant 24/7 · Status: prove with a pilot

3 · Tokenized municipal bond pilot (small, for precedent)

A small tranche to test settlement & retail access. Quincy, MA ($10M, 2024) removed the paying agent and settled near-instantly—but published no dollar savings and hasn't repeated; Lugano (CHF 320M) is stronger but runs on Swiss rails the US lacks. Value is access & precedent, not a savings number.

Fee saving: bps on par · Strategic: medium · Feasibility: medium-low

Don't anchor on the wrong number

After 95+ primary sources and a 3-vote refutation gate, the "switch to crypto and save billions" claim does not survive scrutiny. Do not anchor any pilot on transaction-fee or idle-cash savings—the big flows already ride ACH/Fedwire at near-zero cost and idle cash already earns ~5%. The City's leverage is owning the rail and the relationship, and most margin accrues to payers and businesses, not a line-item budget cut.

Portfolio recommendation

Lead with #1—the one place real interchange margin exists and scales with ticket size. Run #2 as a measured ACH-alternative pilot proving instant/programmable settlement on its own merits. Use #3 for precedent—and be straight that the win is access, not budget savings.

Part II · The Bigger Prize

Beyond Tokenization: Reclaiming the Financing Rail

Pilots are the on-ramp. The structural prize is bigger: every dollar of public infrastructure is borrowed through a chain of private intermediaries, and the credit is chained to appropriations and rating-agency judgment—not the city's democratic choices.

0.74–3.10%
Cost to issue a muni bond (% of par)—mostly underwriter, advisory, legal & rating fees
$7.4B / $9.4B
NYC Building Aid bonds outstanding vs statutory cap (~79%—running out of room)
~$3.4B/yr
NYC schools' debt service—about 8% of the DOE budget

What blockchain can & can't remove

  • Can remove: the paying agent, the pre-issuance depository step, multi-day settlement lag (→ atomic T+0), and large minimums (via fractionalization).
  • Can't remove: the legal issuance, securities-law disclosure, bond counsel, advisory structuring, and—in every live deal so far—the underwriter and rating dependence.

In Quincy, JPMorgan was both platform and underwriter—the intermediary was internalized, not abolished. Tokenization alone re-platforms the straitjacket; it doesn't loosen it.

The documented levers—disintermediate, don't re-platform

  • Public collective agencies (LGFA model): many municipalities co-own one AAA issuer that borrows collectively and on-lends. New Zealand's LGFA (80% council-owned) holds AAA vs councils' AA (~2 notches up) and funds ~90% of council borrowing at tighter spreads. Peers: Kommuninvest (SE), MuniFin (FI), Agence France Locale, JFM (JP).
  • Direct subsidy: Build America Bonds' 35% federal interest subsidy cut borrowing costs ~35–65 bps (caveat: sequestration later clawed part back).

The synthesis: a tokenized public bond bank

Bolt pilot #3's rails onto a member-owned public issuer—programmable use-of-proceeds + atomic settlement + fractional retail access on a publicly-owned vehicle. That's where "go direct to your own citizens, cut out the global banks" becomes structurally true rather than re-platformed.

Sources: MSRB/GFOA cost-of-issuance; NYC Comptroller & Fitch (TFA/BARB, 2025); David Backer, Paying for It (The Baffler, 2025); New Zealand LGFA; Brookings/NBER on Build America Bonds; JPMorgan Kinexys / Bond Buyer (Quincy). Compiled via multi-agent deep research with 3-vote verification.
