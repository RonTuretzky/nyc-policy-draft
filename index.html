Introduction

About Decentral Park NYC

A collective of crypto-native technologists, researchers, and community organizers exploring how blockchain technology can serve progressive ends—not corporate interests.

Who We Are

Our members include industry professionals, academics, developers, and community organizers with experience in blockchain technology—all united by the belief that crypto should serve communities, not just investors. We've seen blockchain used for speculation and extraction. We want to reclaim it for democratic participation, privacy preservation, and building alternatives to extractive financial systems.

Our Approach

Crypto has well-documented problems that this document does not attempt to address. Instead, we focus on a specific question: what would crypto policy look like if designed for public benefit rather than industry promotion? We critique approaches that prioritize corporate interests and propose alternatives grounded in genuine community governance.

The Case for Crypto

Why Blockchain Matters for Democratizing Technology

Crypto isn't just about investment or speculation. At its core, blockchain technology enables infrastructure that no single entity controls—and that's a powerful tool for communities.

Already Happening in NYC

In 2023, RadicalxChange partnered with Harlem District 9 to pilot quadratic voting for participatory budgeting—the first time a US public official used online blockchain-verified voting open to their whole constituency. 80% satisfaction rate.

The Problem with Centralized Tech

  • Payment rails: Visa/Mastercard extract 2-3% of all commerce
  • Platform dependency: Banks can freeze accounts, apps can be shut down
  • Surveillance: Every transaction tracked, shared with law enforcement
  • Financial exclusion: 1.4 billion adults globally unbanked; many more underbanked

What Blockchain Enables

  • Permissionless access: No gatekeepers deciding who can participate
  • Transparent governance: Decisions recorded on public ledger
  • Privacy options: Transactions without surveillance
  • Censorship resistance: No single point of control or failure

"Crypto is infrastructure, not just investment. Like roads or water systems, payment rails and governance tools should be public goods—not controlled by corporations extracting value from every transaction."

A Trust Layer for Government

Rather than building new centralized platforms, blockchain can embed transparency and accountability directly into how public rules and finances operate. This creates "credibility environments" around government claims—without adding new bureaucratic overhead. The goal: extend accountability from administrative processes to transparent technical mechanisms that citizens can verify independently.

Guiding Framework

Our Core Principles

Every recommendation in this document is guided by these questions:

👥

Who Benefits?

Residents or industry? Low-income New Yorkers or tech investors? Programs should serve the public.

🎯

Does It Solve a Real Problem?

Or is this a solution looking for a problem? Technology for its own sake wastes public resources.

⚖️

Would Simpler Tech Work?

Blockchain is rarely the best tool. Before adding complexity, ask if conventional tech would serve just as well.

🔒

What Are the Privacy Risks?

Who has access to data? Can it be shared with police or ICE? Surveillance must be prevented by design.

🗳️

Who Has a Seat at the Table?

Just industry, or also labor, tenants, consumers? Democratic participation must be structural.

💰

What's the Opportunity Cost?

Could these funds better serve residents another way? Every dollar on industry promotion is a dollar not spent on housing.

Program 1

Office of Digital Assets & Blockchain Reduce Budget

Created October 14, 2025 via Executive Order 57. Primary recommendation: reduce the budget significantly and redirect remaining resources toward community use cases rather than industry promotion. Est. budget: $736K - $1.79M annually.

Budget Methodology

Estimate based on comparable NYC mayoral office staffing: Executive Director ($180-220K), 2 Policy Analysts ($140-180K), Admin ($65-85K), plus 35% benefits. Operating costs include office space within OTI, events/outreach, and consulting. Benchmarked against Mayor's Office of Climate & Media offices.

Sources: OTI FY25 Budget, NYC Payroll Data, Executive Order 57

Current Problems

  • Mission focused on industry promotion not residents
  • Emphasis on "world's hub" and "attract talent"
  • High-finance use cases prioritized

Suggested New Missions (if retained)

  • Community currencies for local economies
  • Mutual aid tools for neighborhood resilience
  • Cooperative infrastructure for worker ownership
  • Financial inclusion for unbanked residents

Recommended Path

Reduce budget significantly — cut industry promotion activities and redirect remaining resources toward community-focused initiatives. Amend EO 57 to shift from high-finance speculation to social utility. Partner with community organizations, not Wall Street.

Day 1 Actions

  • Reduce budget significantly — cut industry promotion spending
  • Amend EO 57 — rewrite mission statement
Program 2

NYC Crypto Advisory Council Reform

Currently 150+ executives focused on speculation and industry promotion. Reform to balance expertise across different crypto applications—not just finance, but also public goods and community infrastructure. Est. budget: $90K - $340K annually.

Budget Methodology

Advisory councils have low direct costs (unpaid members) but require staff support. Estimate: Council Coordinator (0.5-1 FTE: $45-110K), event costs ($15-75K), meeting expenses (4-6/year: $10-40K), communications/legal. Benchmarked against comparable NYC industry advisory bodies.

Sources: CoinDesk Advisory Council, NYC Tech Talent Pipeline

Current Problems

  • Narrow expertise: Heavy on trading/finance, lacking in governance, identity, infrastructure
  • Speculation focus: Emphasis on investment over social utility applications
  • No transparency: No published membership, no meeting minutes

Reformed Expertise Mix

  • Public goods builders: Gitcoin, Protocol Labs, regenerative crypto experts
  • Governance researchers: Quadratic voting, DAOs, participatory mechanisms
  • Infrastructure devs: Payment rails, identity systems, municipal applications
  • Industry (balanced): Retain some finance expertise, add social impact focus

Day 1 Action

Issue executive order reforming council to broaden expertise beyond speculation. Add crypto experts working on public goods, governance mechanisms, and municipal infrastructure. Shift mandate from "industry promotion" to "advising on beneficial applications." Publish membership roster and meeting minutes.

Program 4

NYC Node Program Maintain

Ethereum node at CUNY Queens College with educational curriculum. The best of existing crypto initiatives—low cost, public institution, actual workforce development. Est. budget: $74K - $232K annually.

Budget Methodology

Sources: NYCEDC NYC Node, Ethereum Staking, CUNY Salary Schedules

What It Does

  • Runs Ethereum validator node at CUNY

Program 7

Crypto Tax Payments Cancel

Proposed but never implemented. Similar programs elsewhere have seen negligible adoption—Colorado received only 78 payments totaling ~$57,000 over 3 years (0.0005% of state tax revenue). Utah has received zero payments since 2023 launch.

Budget Methodology (If Implemented)

Benchmarked against Colorado's program (2022): payment processor integration ($200-600K), DOF system modifications ($150-500K), security/compliance ($100-350K), training ($50-150K), ongoing fees/support ($250-550K). Colorado had <80 total payments in 3 years—cost-benefit is questionable.

Sources: Colorado Crypto Tax, Ohio Program (defunct), Miami MiamiCoin

Why These Programs Fail

  • Capital gains tax: Spending crypto triggers taxable event
  • Economic disincentive: Exchange fees (1-2%) higher than free ACH
  • Holder mentality: People view crypto as investment, not payment
  • Opportunity cost: Strong returns discourage spending

Documented Failures

  • Colorado: 78 payments, $57K in 3 years
  • Utah: Zero payments since Jan 2023
  • Ohio: Program suspended 2019
  • Cost vs benefit: $850K-$2.5M to implement for negligible use

Recommendation: Cancel

Do not implement. Every comparable program has seen negligible adoption. Colorado's $57,000 in 3 years doesn't justify an $850K-$2.5M implementation cost. Focus city resources on programs with proven demand instead of chasing crypto headlines.

Program 9

NYC Blockchain Plan Pause

Released December 2025 by OTI. We are not convinced this is a good use case for crypto. Asbestos certs, vital records, and property deeds can all be enshrined in publicly verifiable ways without blockchain. Pilot costs TBD.

Budget Methodology

Specific costs not yet published. Based on comparable government blockchain pilots: development ($300K-1M), integration ($500K-1.5M), security audits ($150-400K), per pilot. Dubai Land Registry ($3-5M), Cook County IL property records (~$2M), Estonia e-Residency ($10M+). Each NYC pilot should require cost-benefit analysis.

Sources: NYC OTI, DL News, Estonia e-Residency

Core Skepticism

  • Asbestos Certs — conventional databases can do this
  • Vital Records — non-blockchain public verification exists
  • Property Deeds — existing systems with digital signatures work
  • City Payments — low demand, unnecessary complexity

Salvageable Elements

  • Establishing evaluation criteria is a good direction
  • General approach could work IF community input is added
  • Could improve IF reframed to be NYC-focused, not industry-only

Recommendation: Freeze This Program

Pause all pilots if possible. These use cases do not clearly require blockchain — publicly verifiable data can be achieved through conventional cryptographic methods. If the program continues, require community input, NYC-focused framing, and rigorous alternatives analysis.

Day 1 Actions

  • Freeze all pilots pending review
  • Require alternatives analysis — prove blockchain is necessary
  • If continued: add community input, NYC-focused framing
  • Retain evaluation criteria requirement for any proposals

Why Cities?

Per the Ethereum Foundation Use Case Lab:

  • Proximity: Cities are where coordination and service delivery must happen in real time, with proximity to concerned residents
  • Pragmatism: Cities face immediate service demands requiring practical, not theoretical, solutions
  • Replicability: Thousands of municipalities face common operational needs—solutions can scale across cities
  • Leverage: Cities are powerful levers for public sector innovation, offering scalable pathways for modernizing civic capacity
Proposal 1

Participatory Budgeting

Use blockchain-verified voting and quadratic mechanisms to give New Yorkers real decision-making power over public resources—from neighborhood budgets to pension investments.

Proven in NYC: Harlem District 9

In 2023, RadicalxChange and Council Member Kristin Richardson Jordan ran the first US quadratic voting participatory budget—online, blockchain-verified, open to the whole constituency. 80% satisfaction rate. Top-funded: Baxter affordable housing ($1M), Marcus Garvey Playground ($275K).

Why Quadratic Voting?

Traditional voting lets wealthy interests dominate. Quadratic voting gives everyone voice credits they can spread across issues—caring deeply costs more, so broad consensus wins over narrow intensity.

  • Anti-plutocracy: Prevents whales from buying outcomes
  • Amplifies consensus: Broad support beats narrow intensity
  • Delegation: Voters can delegate to trusted community reps

Applications for NYC

  • Community Board budgets: Let residents vote on neighborhood spending
  • Pension fund allocation: Workers vote on how their $250B+ is invested
  • Capital projects: Community input on infrastructure priorities
  • Grant distribution: Quadratic funding for community organizations

Global Examples

  • Moscow Active Citizen: 5M+ users, blockchain voting since 2017
  • Barcelona Decidim: Open-source platform, 150+ cities

Implementation

Scale the Harlem pilot citywide. Partner with RadicalxChange for methodology, use existing tools (Snapshot, Tally) adapted for municipal use.

Proposal 2

City Currency System

A stablecoin-based municipal payment system that captures value currently extracted by Visa, Mastercard, and other payment processors—redirecting all fees directly into city funding.

Value Being Captured Today

  • 2-3% transaction fees on every card payment in NYC
  • Billions annually flowing to Visa, Mastercard, payment processors
  • Merchant fees passed on as higher prices for residents
  • Zero city benefit from payment infrastructure it enables

Municipal Alternatives Exist

  • Seoul T-money: City-owned payment system (34% municipal ownership), used for transit, retail, and services
  • Lugano, Switzerland: Accepts Bitcoin/USDT for all municipal taxes and services via "Plan B"

Stablecoin Municipal Model

  • NYC Stablecoin: 1:1 USD-backed municipal currency
  • Yield-bearing: Treasury reserves generate interest for city budget
  • Replace credit card rails: Merchants save 2-3% on every transaction
  • Capture all fees: Transaction fees fund city services, not Wall Street
  • Instant settlement: No 3-day wait for merchant deposits

Key Insight

The city already enables billions in commerce through its infrastructure. A municipal stablecoin lets NYC capture the value of its own economy—transaction fees become city revenue, treasury reserves earn yield, and residents benefit from lower merchant costs.
